\begin{abstract}
We revisit three published high-velocity-star catalogues drawn from
Gaia and LAMOST -- \citet[591 stars]{Li2021HVS591},
\citet[88 stars]{Li2023ExtremeVelocity88} and
\citet[519 stars]{Liao2024HVS519} -- and ask a single question: how
much of the published unbound classification depends on the distance
estimator, and how much on the radial velocity catalogue?
After Gaia DR3 source\_id resolution and de-duplication our master
sample contains 1101 unique stars. 675 of them satisfy
$\mathrm{ruwe}<1.4$, $\varpi/\sigma_\varpi>5$ and carry a Gaia DR3
radial velocity (the Gaia-only clean sample). 356 additionally pass a
1\,arcsec LAMOST DR9 LRS cross-match, atmospheric-parameter cuts and a
50\,km\,s$^{-1}$ Gaia--LAMOST RV agreement test (the final-strict
sample).
We then compute the three-dimensional Galactocentric rest-frame speed
$\vgrf$ and the local escape speed $\vesc$ for every star in the
final-strict sample, propagating errors with a 1000-draw Monte Carlo
under the \textsc{MWPotential2014} potential of \citet{Bovy2015galpy},
once with inverse-parallax distances ($1000/\varpi$) and once with the
geometric distances of \citet{BailerJones2021Distances}.
Switching from inverse parallax to Bailer-Jones distances on the same
356 stars reduces the count with $\Punb = P(\vgrf>\vesc)>0.5$ from
\textbf{48 to 3}, and the count with $\Punb>0.9$ from \textbf{12 to 1};
45 stars are downgraded across the $\Punb=0.5$ threshold and none are
gained. Substituting LAMOST radial velocities for Gaia radial
velocities on the same sample changes the median $\vgrf$ by
$0.00\kmps$ ($p_{90}|\Delta\vgrf|=5.7\kmps$) and flips the
$\Punb=0.5$ classification of one star out of 356. We conclude that the
unbound classification of these published candidates is set by the
distance estimator, not by the choice of radial-velocity catalogue. The
final-strict sample, a Top-15 main candidate table, and a Top-30
machine-readable supplementary CSV are released with this paper; the
three highest-confidence candidates are metal-poor giants that warrant
targeted spectroscopic follow-up.
\keywords{high-velocity stars -- Galaxy: kinematics and dynamics --
stars: distances -- catalogues -- methods: data analysis}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

High-velocity stars (HVS) trace the most extreme end of Galactic stellar
kinematics. The classical picture of \citet{Hills1988HVS} predicts that
tidal disruption of close binaries by the central massive black hole
ejects stars at speeds well above the local Galactic escape speed; this
mechanism was placed on a firm theoretical footing by
\citet{YuTremaine2003Ejection} and observationally established by
\citet{Brown2005FirstHVS}, who reported the first unbound HVS in the Milky
Way halo. The subsequent decade-and-a-half of dedicated surveys
\citep[][and references therein]{Brown2007BoundHVS,Kenyon2008HVSGCtoHalo,
Brown2014MMTSurvey} consolidated HVSs as a distinct dynamical class,
reviewed comprehensively by \citet{Brown2015HVSReview}.

The advent of Gaia astrometry transformed the HVS landscape. Both
\citet{Boubert2018Revisiting} and \citet{Marchetti2019Fastest} revisited
literature HVS catalogues using Gaia DR2 phase-space information and showed
that the published unbound classification of many candidates depended
sensitively on the assumed distance scale and on the propagation of
parallax uncertainties. The combination of Gaia DR3 with LAMOST DR9
\citep{Cui2012LAMOST,Yan2022LAMOSTDecade} now provides 6D phase-space
coverage with self-consistent atmospheric parameters for a substantial
fraction of bright candidates, and three recent VizieR-published catalogues
\citep{Li2021HVS591,Li2023ExtremeVelocity88,Liao2024HVS519} compile a
combined 1198 candidate rows that are the natural starting point for any
contemporary reassessment.

The interpretation of these candidates as bound or unbound, however,
hinges on the choice of distance estimator. Inverse parallax, $1/\varpi$,
is biased toward larger distances and increased random errors precisely in
the high-velocity regime where parallax signal-to-noise is small
\citep{BailerJones2015Distances,Luri2018GaiaParallaxes,Lindegren2021ParallaxBias}.
The geometric-distance catalogue of \citet{BailerJones2021Distances},
which folds in a Galactic prior, returns systematically smaller distances
for parallax-uncertain stars and is now the standard product for kinematic
work.

In this paper we re-derive the unbound classification of the three
published catalogues under both distance estimators, propagating
uncertainties through a 1000-draw Monte Carlo sampler, and we test the
robustness of the conclusion against the choice of radial velocity
catalogue (Gaia DR3 vs.\ LAMOST DR9). The comparison is performed on a
fixed sample of 356 stars -- the final-strict sample defined in
Section~\ref{sec:cuts} -- so that the inverse-parallax and Bailer-Jones
passes share identical proper motions, radial velocities,
LAMOST-quality cuts and Monte Carlo error budgets. Any change in the
unbound count therefore reflects the distance estimator, and not the
sample selection.
On this same 356-star sample, $\Punb>0.5$ falls from 48 to 3 and
$\Punb>0.9$ falls from 12 to 1 when we switch from inverse parallax to
Bailer-Jones distances. The radial-velocity choice has almost no
effect: the median $\vgrf$ shifts by $0\kmps$
($p_{90}|\Delta\vgrf|=5.7\kmps$). We conclude that the published
unbound classification is set by the distance estimator.

This paper sits next to, but does not duplicate, the recent CAOSP study
by \citet{Elsanhoury2025CAOSPKinematics}, which used a similar
combination of Gaia DR3 and LAMOST DR9 high-velocity-star catalogues to
derive Galactic kinematic parameters: velocity ellipsoids, the solar
motion and the Oort constants. Our question is different. We do not
recompute $U$, $V$, $W$ statistics or the Oort constants, and we do not
report a new candidate sample. Instead, we fix the same three published
catalogues, freeze the cross-match and quality cuts, and test only one
methodological knob: the distance estimator entering the unbound
probability. The two studies are therefore complementary -- the
\citeauthor{Elsanhoury2025CAOSPKinematics} paper characterises the
kinematic distribution of high-velocity stars; we characterise the
sensitivity of their unbound classification to the distance prescription.

The paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the
sample construction. Section~\ref{sec:methods} details the kinematic
methods, Bailer-Jones distance upgrade, and the Monte Carlo error
propagation. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the results, anchored on
seven figures and three tables. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the
distance-driven downgrade, the physical context of the surviving Top-3
candidates, and the limitations of the analysis. Section~\ref{sec:conclusions}
concludes.

\section{Data and sample construction}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Source catalogues}
\label{sec:catalogues}

We start from three published high-velocity-star candidate catalogues,
all served through VizieR \citep{Ochsenbein2000VizieR} and downloaded with
\textsc{astroquery} \citep{Ginsburg2019Astroquery}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{li2021}: \texttt{J/ApJS/252/3/hivelscs}, the
        \citet{Li2021HVS591} catalogue of 591 high-velocity-star
        candidates selected from LAMOST DR7 and Gaia DR2.
  \item \textbf{li2023}: \texttt{J/AJ/166/12/table5}, the 88-row Table~A1
        of \citet{Li2023ExtremeVelocity88}; we deliberately use the full
        Table~A1 rather than the 52-row escape-velocity-filtered subset
        (\texttt{table2}) as our parent sample.
  \item \textbf{liao2024}: \texttt{J/AJ/167/76/table6}, the 519-star
        catalogue of \citet{Liao2024HVS519} that incorporates the
        gravitational impact of the Large Magellanic Cloud.
\end{itemize}

The combined raw input is 1198 catalogue rows.

\subsection{Gaia DR3 enrichment}
\label{sec:gaia}

For every catalogue source we resolve the Gaia DR3 source\_id via VizieR
and run an asynchronous TAP upload-join against \texttt{gaiadr3.gaia\_source}
\citep{GaiaDR3Summary2023} to retrieve \texttt{parallax},
\texttt{parallax\_error}, $\mu_{\alpha*}$, $\mu_\delta$ and their errors, the
Gaia radial velocity \texttt{radial\_velocity} with its error,
\texttt{phot\_g\_mean\_mag}, \texttt{phot\_bp\_mean\_mag}, \texttt{phot\_rp\_mean\_mag},
and \texttt{ruwe}. Ten of the 591 li2021 source\_ids do not resolve in DR3
(merged or removed between EDR3 and DR3). Combined across the three
catalogues, we obtain 1188 successful Gaia DR3 matches; deduplication
across catalogues yields 1101 unique source\_ids that constitute the
master sample.

\subsection{LAMOST DR9 cross-match}
\label{sec:lamost}

We use the public LAMOST DR9 LRS stellar parameter catalogue, which
contains 6\,921\,466 rows after sentinel-value cleanup. Atmospheric
parameters and radial velocities are produced by the LSP3 pipeline
\citep{Xiang2015LSP3}; the survey design and decadal status are described
in \citet{Zhao2012LAMOSTSurvey,Deng2012LEGUE,Yan2022LAMOSTDecade}.
Cross-matching the 1101 master sources against this catalogue with
\texttt{astropy.coordinates.SkyCoord} \citep{Astropy2013,Astropy2018} at
1\,arcsec yields 928 Gaia--LAMOST pairs (multi-epoch) and 651 unique Gaia
sources matched. For multi-epoch matches we retain the highest-snrg
record per Gaia source. The full angular separation distribution has
median 0.31\,arcsec and 99\% percentile 0.91\,arcsec, well within the
match radius.

\subsection{Quality cuts and analysis samples}
\label{sec:cuts}

We define the following Boolean flags
\citep[motivated by][]{Lindegren2021ParallaxBias,Luri2018GaiaParallaxes}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{q\_gaia\_astrometry}: $\mathrm{ruwe}<1.4$ AND
        $\varpi>0$ AND $\varpi/\sigma_\varpi>5$.
  \item \texttt{q\_gaia\_rv}: Gaia DR3 radial velocity available.
  \item \texttt{q\_lamost}: 1\,arcsec LAMOST cross-match success.
  \item \texttt{q\_lamost\_quality}: \texttt{q\_lamost} AND $T_\mathrm{eff}$,
        $\log g$, [Fe/H] all reported AND continuum $S/N \geq 20$.
  \item \texttt{q\_rv\_consistent}: both Gaia and LAMOST RV reported,
        $|\Delta v_{\rm RV}|\leq 50\kmps$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{sloppypar}
We define two analysis populations. The \emph{Gaia-only clean} sample is
the conjunction of \texttt{q\_gaia\_astrometry} and \texttt{q\_gaia\_rv}
(N$=$675), used as the kinematic background. The \emph{final strict}
sample additionally requires \texttt{q\_lamost\_quality} and
\texttt{q\_rv\_consistent} (N$=$356) and is the population on which all
final candidate statistics are based. Two stars have $|\Delta v_{\rm RV}|>50\kmps$
but otherwise pass all gates; we keep them in the master and report
them separately as RV-outlier follow-up candidates rather than including
them in the final strict.
\end{sloppypar}

The full sample-construction funnel is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:funnel} and tabulated in Table~\ref{tab:funnel}.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Coordinate frame and solar parameters}
\label{sec:frame}

\begin{sloppypar}
All kinematic quantities are computed in the Galactocentric frame
implemented in \texttt{astropy.coordinates}
\citep{Astropy2013,Astropy2018}. We adopt $R_\odot = 8.122$\,kpc
\citep{Gravity2018R0}, $z_\odot = 0.0208$\,kpc
\citep{BennettBovy2019Zsun}, and a solar Galactocentric Cartesian
velocity of $(12.9,\,245.6,\,7.78)\kmps$, drawn from
\citet{ReidBrunthaler2020SolarMotion} for the absolute component and
from \citet{Schoenrich2010LSR} for the local peculiar component.
\end{sloppypar}
The $(U,V,W)$ components reported in
Fig.~\ref{fig:toomre} and the supplementary Top-30 CSV are heliocentric
Galactic
Cartesian, with $U$ positive toward the Galactic centre, $V$ positive in
the direction of Galactic rotation, and $W$ positive toward the North
Galactic Pole; they are not LSR-corrected. The Galactocentric speed
$\vgrf$ and the planar local escape speed $\vesc(\Rgc)$ are evaluated
under \textsc{MWPotential2014} \citep{Bovy2015galpy}; alternative
potentials of comparable parameter quality
\citep{McMillan2017MWMass,Piffl2014RAVEvesc,Monari2018Vesc} change
$\vesc(R_\odot)$ at the few-percent level and are folded into the
discussion of systematics in Sect.~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Distance estimators}
\label{sec:distance}

We use two distance estimators. The \emph{preliminary} distance is the
inverse-parallax distance $d_\varpi=1000/\varpi$\,pc, with all the
well-known biases for parallax-uncertain stars
\citep{BailerJones2015Distances,Luri2018GaiaParallaxes}. The
\emph{primary} distance is the Bailer-Jones geometric distance
$\dBJ$ from \citet{BailerJones2021Distances}, queried as the
\texttt{r\_med\_geo} column of the \texttt{external.gaiaedr3\_distance}
table on the Gaia archive; we also retrieve the 16th and 84th percentiles
$r_{\mathrm{lo,geo}}$ and $r_{\mathrm{hi,geo}}$ for Monte Carlo sampling.
All 1101 master sources match the Bailer-Jones catalogue successfully;
EDR3$\leftrightarrow$DR3 source\_id compatibility is 100\% in our sample.
Figure~\ref{fig:distance} compares the two estimators directly and is
discussed in Sect.~\ref{sec:results-distance}.

\subsection{Monte Carlo error propagation}
\label{sec:mc}

For each star we draw $N_{\mathrm{MC}}=1000$ jointly sampled realizations
of $(\varpi$ \emph{or} $\dBJ)$, $\mu_{\alpha*}$, $\mu_\delta$, and the
adopted radial velocity. Distances under the inverse-parallax pass are
sampled from a normal $\mathcal{N}(\varpi,\sigma_\varpi)$ truncated at
$\varpi>0$. Distances under the BJ pass are sampled log-normally with
$\sigma_{\log d}=(\log_{10}r_{\mathrm{hi,geo}}-\log_{10}r_{\mathrm{lo,geo}})/2$
to handle the typical asymmetry of the BJ percentiles. Proper motions and
RV are sampled from independent normals with their reported errors. For
each draw we compute $(U,V,W)$, $\vgrf$, $\Vtot$, the local escape speed
$\vesc(\Rgc)$, and the ratio $\vgrf/\vesc$; the unbound probability is
the MC fraction with $\vgrf>\vesc$.

\subsection{Radial-velocity sensitivity check}
\label{sec:rvcheck}

On the final-strict sample we re-run the kinematic solution with the
LAMOST radial velocity replacing the Gaia DR3 value, keeping every other
input identical. We label the two resulting estimates
$\vgrf^{\mathrm{Gaia}}$ and $\vgrf^{\mathrm{LAMOST}}$; their per-star
difference $\Delta\vgrf = \vgrf^{\mathrm{LAMOST}} - \vgrf^{\mathrm{Gaia}}$
underpins Sect.~\ref{sec:rvresults} and Fig.~\ref{fig:rvsens}.

\label{sec:sky}

\subsection{Distance reassessment}
\label{sec:results-distance}

Figure~\ref{fig:distance} compares inverse-parallax and Bailer-Jones
distances on the master sample. Bailer-Jones distances are systematically
smaller, with median $\dBJ/(1000/\varpi)=0.88$ across the master sample
and $0.76$ on the 50 candidates with the largest preliminary $\Punb$
(red triangles). For tangentially-dominated objects the implied total
peculiar speed scales linearly with distance; the 24\% distance shrinkage
on the Top-50 therefore translates directly into a $\sim 24\%$ reduction
in $\vgrf$, which is enough to push many candidates below the local
$\vesc$. Five li2023 stars in the master have $\varpi<0$; the
Bailer-Jones catalogue gives them physically sensible distances of
2--5\,kpc.

The headline impact on the unbound classification is summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}. \emph{All counts in
Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity} are computed on the same final-strict sample
of 356 stars; the two passes differ only in the distance estimator.}
On this fixed sample, the count of likely-unbound stars
($\Punb>0.5$) drops from 48 (inverse parallax) to 3 (Bailer-Jones), and
the count of high-confidence unbound stars ($\Punb>0.9$) drops from 12 to
1. Of the 48 stars classified as $\Punb>0.5$ under inverse parallax, 45
are downgraded across the 0.5 threshold by the Bailer-Jones pass and 3
remain; no star is gained by switching estimators. The reduction is
therefore not a side-effect of the LAMOST quality or RV-consistency cuts
that define the final-strict sample, but a direct consequence of the
choice of distance estimator.

\subsection{Radial-velocity sensitivity}
\label{sec:rvresults}

Figure~\ref{fig:rvsens} presents the radial-velocity sensitivity check on
the final-strict sample. Per-star $\vgrf$ from Gaia DR3 and LAMOST DR9
radial velocities (panel a) lie almost exactly on the $y=x$ line; the
distribution of the difference $\Delta\vgrf=\mathrm{LAMOST}-\mathrm{Gaia}$
(panel b) has median $0.00\kmps$, $p_{90}|\Delta|=5.7\kmps$ and
$\max|\Delta|=32.0\kmps$. Of the 356 final-strict stars only one
flips $\Punb$ across the 0.5 threshold between the two RV passes. The
LAMOST DR9 LRS radial velocities are known to carry an
$\approx -5.7\kmps$ zero-point offset relative to Gaia DR3 in our matched
subset; because we propagate uncertainties through the Monte Carlo,
this offset does not propagate to a meaningful change in the headline
classification.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why most preliminary candidates are downgraded}
\label{sec:why-downgraded}

The 24\% median distance shrinkage of the Bailer-Jones estimator on the
inverse-parallax-flagged unbound subset (Sect.~\ref{sec:results-distance})
is sufficient to explain the same-sample drop from 48 to 3 stars with
$\Punb>0.5$.
For tangentially dominated stars the inferred peculiar speed scales
linearly with distance; combined with proper-motion uncertainties at the
0.05--0.5\,mas\,yr$^{-1}$ level a 24\% distance reduction translates to
$O(150\kmps)$ change in $\Vtot$, comparable to the $\vesc$--$\Vtot$
margin in the high-velocity tail. Our results are consistent with the
Gaia-DR2-era reassessment of \citet{Boubert2018Revisiting} and with the
Gaia-DR2 fastest-stars analysis of \citet{Marchetti2019Fastest}, both of
which highlighted the strong dependence of unbound classifications on the
distance prescription.

\subsection{The Top-3 candidates}
\label{sec:top3}

The three highest-confidence unbound candidates in the final strict
sample are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Rank 1}: Gaia DR3 source\_id \texttt{1383279090527227264},
        present in both \citet{Li2021HVS591} and \citet{Liao2024HVS519};
        $d_{\mathrm{BJ}}=6.39$\,kpc, $\overline{\vgrf}=653\pm 55\kmps$,
        $\Punb=0.996$; LAMOST atmospheric parameters
        $T_\mathrm{eff}=4801$\,K, $\log g=1.88$, $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]=-1.40$,
        i.e.\ a metal-poor giant. Independent inclusion in two literature
        catalogues lends support; recent chemical work on extreme-velocity
        stars \citep{Nelson2024ExtremeVelChem} provides relevant context
        for the population to which this object likely belongs.
  \item \textbf{Rank 2}: Gaia DR3 source\_id \texttt{1204061267883975040}
        (liao2024 only), $d_{\mathrm{BJ}}=9.74$\,kpc,
        $\overline{\vgrf}=612\pm 90\kmps$, $\Punb=0.670$; very metal-poor
        ($[\mathrm{Fe/H}]=-2.38$).
  \item \textbf{Rank 3}: Gaia DR3 source\_id \texttt{3877058564258758656}
        (liao2024 only), $d_{\mathrm{BJ}}=5.51$\,kpc,
        $\overline{\vgrf}=498\pm 50\kmps$, $\Punb=0.571$; metal-poor
        giant ($T_\mathrm{eff}=5143$\,K, $\log g=2.29$,
        $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]=-1.21$).
\end{itemize}
All three are giant or sub-giant late-type stars with metallicities
typical of the Galactic halo, consistent with the late-type HVS
candidate populations discussed by \citet{Li2020LateTypeHVS} and the
Gaia--LAMOST high-velocity samples of \citet{Du2018LocalHalo,Du2018Origin}.

We checked the three sources against SIMBAD on 2026~April~30. Rank-1 is
catalogued there as UCAC4~656-058031, with the SIMBAD position imported
directly from Gaia DR3 (2020yCat.1350); rank-2 is present only as the
Gaia DR3 entry itself; rank-3 has no SIMBAD record within 5\,arcsec of
its Gaia coordinates. None of the three carries a published variability,
binarity or spectroscopic-binary flag at the time of writing. The only
external corroboration we identify is the dual-catalogue inclusion of
rank-1 in both \citet{Li2021HVS591} and \citet{Liao2024HVS519}; this is
also the only object in our sample with $\Punb>0.9$. The absence of
flags is, of course, not equivalent to a confirmation of single-star
status: targeted multi-epoch spectroscopy of the kind reported by
\citet{Hattori2019LAMOSTHVS1} for LAMOST-HVS1 remains the most direct
test. We do not claim discovery; the Top-3 are reassessments of stars
already present in the literature.

\subsection{Gaia versus LAMOST radial velocities}
\label{sec:rvdiscuss}

The median difference $\mathrm{LAMOST}-\mathrm{Gaia}=-5.66\kmps$ in the
matched subset (Sect.~\ref{sec:rvresults}) reflects the well-documented
LAMOST DR9 LRS zero-point offset. We have not applied a zero-point
correction in the primary kinematic solution, because the headline
classification is insensitive to it: the median $\Delta\vgrf$ on the
final-strict sample is $0.00\kmps$, the $p_{90}$ absolute deviation is
$5.7\kmps$, and the chemical tagging arguments of
\citet{Hawkins2018ChemicalTagging} and \citet{Bromley2018NearbyHighSpeed}
also do not require sub-km\,s$^{-1}$ RV accuracy.

\subsection{Limitations and follow-up}
\label{sec:limitations}

Four systematics affect the headline numbers. The first is the
escape-speed approximation: $\vesc$ is evaluated in the disk plane
($z=0$), and off-plane corrections under \textsc{MWPotential2014} are at
the few-percent level. Because rank-2 and rank-3 sit close to the
unbound boundary at $\Punb=0.670$ and $0.571$, their classification can
flip under alternative potentials such as those of
\citet{McMillan2017MWMass} and \citet{Piffl2014RAVEvesc,Monari2018Vesc}.
Only rank-1 ($\Punb=0.996$) survives such variations as a robust
unbound candidate; ranks 2 and 3 should be treated as marginal pending
external confirmation. The second concerns the Bailer-Jones prior: it
is tuned to the Galactic disk and may itself shrink the distances of
genuine halo high-velocity tracers. The 48-to-3 reduction is therefore
prior-dependent rather than a definitive measurement, and we phrase it
that way throughout. The third systematic is sample geometry. LAMOST
coverage is largely restricted to the northern hemisphere and the
\citet{Li2023ExtremeVelocity88} subset contributes zero objects to the
final-strict sample under our cross-match and quality criteria.
Conclusions about atmospheric parameters therefore rest on the
\citet{Li2021HVS591} and \citet{Liao2024HVS519} subsets only. The
fourth is unresolved binarity: it can inflate the apparent radial
velocity, and the two stars with $|\Delta v_{\rm RV}|>50\kmps$ that
otherwise pass our quality gates are the natural targets for
multi-epoch follow-up.

The follow-up programme implied by these results is therefore: (a)
targeted high-resolution spectroscopy of the Top-3 candidates to confirm
or refute their unbound status under a panel of Galactic potentials
\citep[e.g.][]{McMillan2017MWMass,Piffl2014RAVEvesc,Monari2018Vesc};
(b) multi-epoch radial-velocity monitoring of the two RV-outlier
follow-up targets; and (c) re-running the present pipeline with Gaia DR4
parallaxes once they become available---we expect the parallax error
budget to halve and the BJ-vs-inverse-parallax distance gap to narrow.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item On a single fixed final-strict sample of 356 stars drawn from
        three published HVS catalogues
        \citep{Li2021HVS591,Li2023ExtremeVelocity88,Liao2024HVS519},
        replacing inverse-parallax distances with Bailer-Jones geometric
        distances reduces the count of likely-unbound stars
        ($\Punb>0.5$) from \emph{48} to \emph{3} and the count of
        high-confidence unbound stars ($\Punb>0.9$) from \emph{12} to
        \emph{1}. 45 stars are downgraded across $\Punb=0.5$ and none
        are promoted.
  \item The reduction is driven by a systematic distance shrinkage
        of 12\,\% across the strict sample, rising to 24\,\% on the
        subset that the inverse-parallax pass had flagged as $\Punb>0.5$.
        Substituting LAMOST radial velocities for Gaia radial velocities
        on the same sample changes the median $\vgrf$ by $0\kmps$
        ($p_{90}|\Delta|=5.7\kmps$) and flips the $\Punb=0.5$
        classification of only one star. The unbound classification is
        therefore distance-dominated, not RV-dominated.
  \item We release a final-strict sample of 356 stars, a Top-15 main
        candidate table (Table~\ref{tab:top15}) and a supplementary
        Top-30 as a machine-readable CSV. The three highest-confidence
        candidates are the metal-poor giants Gaia DR3
        \texttt{1383279090527227264},\allowbreak{}
        \texttt{1204061267883975040}\allowbreak{} and
        \texttt{3877058564258758656}; targeted spectroscopic follow-up
        is recommended.
  \item Two stars with
        $|\Delta_{\mathrm{rv}}\,(\mathrm{LAMOST}-\mathrm{Gaia})|>50\kmps$
        are flagged as binary or variable-RV candidates; we do not
        include them in the unbound headline list and report them
        separately as follow-up targets.
\end{enumerate}

scripts, the Top-30 machine-readable supplementary CSV, and the LaTeX
source of this manuscript are publicly available at
\url{https://github.com/Harry33t/caosp-hivel-pipeline} under the MIT
license.
